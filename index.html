💳How Parents Pay — Billing Mechanics

One fixed monthly Direct Debit. Day rates are a pricing and benchmarking unit — not what appears on the invoice. Below is the full calculation mechanics including a worked example.

📆
What parents receive: one monthly DD
One fixed invoice per month, paid in advance. Same amount every month regardless of bank holidays or working days. Accepted via Direct Debit, Tax-Free Childcare, or childcare vouchers. Part-week families pay proportionally less — a 3d/week place has a lower monthly DD than 5d/week, based on committed days × day rate × 51wks ÷ 12.
(Day rate × committed days/wk × 51wks) ÷ 12 = monthly DD
🔢
Standard day = 10 hours (08:00–18:00)
The 07:30–08:00 early start is optional at +£15/day, booked and paid in advance. The base day rate covers 08:00–18:00. The hourly rate is derived from the day rate: it's used to calculate funded deductions and part-day bookings, not as a standalone fee.
Hourly = Day rate ÷ 10 hours
e.g. £95 ÷ 10h = £9.50/h
⚖️
Government funding: deducted automatically
DfE reimburses the nursery per hour at the funded rate. The parent pays only the private hours above the entitlement plus meals and enrichment. The monthly DD reflects the net private amount only.
Private hrs × hourly rate
+ meals + enrichment = parent DD

💡 Baby Retention Premium — The Compounding Advantage

A baby enrolled at 6–9 months stays for 3–4 years, generating revenue across 4 age groups. A pre-schooler enrolled at 3 years stays only 1–2 years. Linda confirmed: "Once you get the baby in, you have the baby for the next four years."

Staff ratios: Babies 1:3 (high cost) → Pre-School 1:8 (low cost). Baby-to-preschool retention path creates a natural cross-subsidy: baby years build loyalty, preschool years harvest margin. Losing a baby early = losing 3–4 years of future high-margin revenue.

⚖️Portfolio Optimisation — What Mix of Children Should We Target?

This section answers a single question mathematically: given 121 fixed places, what combination of age groups generates the most value? We tested 62,732 combinations and ran sensitivity analysis across multiple scenarios. Below is the result — explained without jargon.

The core insight
The question
We have 121 places. How should we split them across Babies, Toddlers and Pre-School to make the most money — both now and over time?
Why it's not obvious
Pre-School looks cheap to run (1 staff per 8 children vs 1 per 3 for Babies). But babies stay 4 years, pre-schoolers leave in 18 months. The full picture is very different.
The answer
More Babies. Significantly fewer Pre-School. The optimal mix improves monthly profit by +15.5% and lifetime value by +57.9% — and holds up under every scenario we tested.

▶ How are Score and LTV equiv/mth calculated? — click to expand Full methodology with numbers

The Score formula

The Score combines two things that can't naturally be compared — monthly profit (£/month) and 4-year lifetime value (£ over 4 years) — into a single number. Without it, we'd be choosing subjectively between short-term cash and long-term value.

The formula
Score = 0.65 × Monthly Profit + 0.35 × LTV equiv/mth
Why 65/35? A nursery needs short-term cash flow to operate, but is fundamentally a long-term retention business. 65% weight on monthly profit ensures we don't sacrifice cash for theoretical LTV. The 35% LTV weight is enough to tip decisions in favour of Baby places — which have lower monthly margin but vastly higher lifetime value. You can change these weights in the Excel model (cell B71).

What is LTV equiv/mth?

A Baby enrolled at 6 months generates profit over 4 years across 4 rooms. To compare this with monthly profit on equal footing, we convert the 4-year total to a monthly equivalent by dividing by 48.

Does it hold under uncertainty?

The honest answer: we don't know exactly how many children will stay each year, or how fast we'll fill each room. So we tested 6 different "what if" scenarios — from pessimistic (families leave quickly) to optimistic (very loyal families). The optimal mix won every single scenario. In statistics this is called stochastic dominance — it means the result isn't a fluke of our assumptions.

⚠️ The one real risk: filling baby places is harder
Pre-School places fill in 2–3 months (parents decide quickly, strong demand). Baby places take 6–9 months — parents plan during pregnancy, decision cycles are long.

If we shift to 65 Baby places but only fill 70% in Year 1, we carry idle staff costs (you hire for the ratio, not actual occupancy).

Addendum: what if we also optimise prices?

We modelled demand elasticity — how much occupancy falls when we raise prices. Childcare in London is relatively inelastic (parents need care regardless of price). Key finding: raising prices has 10× more impact than shifting room allocations. We tested this across 202,500 price × quantity combinations.

Joint optimum (prices + quantity)
Important caveat on elasticity: these are estimates based on academic literature (IFS 2019). Real elasticity can only be measured by testing. The direction is robust — raise Babies price, hold or modestly raise Pre-School — but the exact magnitude is uncertain. Recommendation: keep current prices at launch, build waitlist evidence, then raise selectively after 6 months when you have real demand data. The quantity optimisation (room mix) is more actionable immediately.
⚠ Full Risk Register — Shifting from 30 to 65 Baby Places

Revenue risk at 70% Baby fill is one of seven material risks. All are manageable — but each requires an active mitigation plan before this mix shift is viable.

Risk 1 · Staffing headcount
+7 qualified staff required (net)
Optimal mix requires 46 staff vs current 39 (net basis). Net +7 headcount. All must be Level 3 qualified nursery practitioners — not casual hires. Recruitment lead time 2–3 months per hire in W5's tight labour market.
Mitigation: begin recruitment 6 months before opening
Risk 2 · Ofsted ratio enforcement
Zero tolerance — one absence = closure risk
Baby room requires exactly 1 staff per 3 children at all times. A single unplanned absence with no cover = ratio breach = Ofsted enforcement, potential suspension of operation. Pre-School (1:8) provides far more buffer.
Mitigation: 20–25% bank/agency staff pool (~£51k/yr additional cost)
Risk 3 · Physical space requirements
+22 sq m net space needed
EYFS requires 3.5 sq m per under-2 child vs 2.5 sq m for older groups. 65 Babies = 228 sq m room space. Pre-School freed = 100 sq m. Net shortfall ~22 sq m. Baby rooms also need: cot area, nappy change stations, bottle prep. These cannot share space with Pre-School layout.
Mitigation: verify with architect before committing to this mix
Risk 4 · Demand acquisition timeline
35 new Baby places need ~6–12 months to fill
Ealing premium Baby market: ~75 qualifying families/year. 35 new places = half the annual market. Baby decisions are made during pregnancy — parents need 6–9 months notice. Pre-School fills in 2–3 months. Year 1 Baby occupancy realistically 55–70%, not 95%.
Mitigation: pre-launch waitlist campaign targeting pregnant parents
Risk 5 · Cash flow transition period
Year 1 profit likely lower than current plan
Dropping 40 Pre-School places removes £29k/mth of certain profit (Pre-School fills fast). Gaining 35 Baby places adds profit only when filled. If Baby at 70% fill: net monthly swing vs current mix = −£5k to −£15k/mth in Year 1. The model pays back in Year 2–3, not Year 1.
Mitigation: model 3-year cashflow, ensure 12-month runway
Risk 6 · Operational expertise gap
Baby room is hardest to operate
Under-2 care requires individual feeding/sleep schedules, higher medical vigilance (allergies, fevers, choking), more anxious parents needing more communication. 65 simultaneous baby settling-in processes vs Pre-School cohort intakes. Need experienced Baby Room Manager — a specific, scarce role.
Mitigation: hire Baby Room Manager before building waitlist
Overall verdict
Don't do immediately: shifting all 40 Pre-School places to Babies on Day 1 creates simultaneous risks across staffing, space, cash flow and demand.
Staged approach: open with current mix, aggressively build Baby waitlist, convert 5–10 Pre-School places to Baby annually as demand proves itself and staff are in place.
The long-run destination is right: the maths on Baby LTV is solid and robust. But it's a 2–3 year journey, not a launch decision.
Recommendation summary
Immediately actionable
1. Build Baby waitlist now — target 40+ families before opening. Fennies near-full is the opportunity.
2. If design not finalised — ask architect for Baby/Baby-Toddler room flexibility: convertible partitions between groups.
3. Launch marketing — lead with under-2 messaging, not Pre-School.
Operational strategy
4. Ramp-up phase — accept more Pre-School in months 1–4 for cash flow, but hold Baby places open.
5. Month 6+ — prioritise Baby waitlist over Pre-School enquiries.
6. Prices — keep current at launch, raise Babies to £115–120 once waitlist validates inelasticity.
